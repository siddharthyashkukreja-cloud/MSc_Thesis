
In the aftermath of the 2007-2008 financial crisis, regulation shifted towards necessitating the use of central clearing counterparties (CCPs) for several types of derivative contracts. Today, CCPs are commonplace even in Cash Equity and Repo markets. The phrase "Too Big To Fail" was used during the crisis for the large US G-SIBs, but is more accurate now in reference to the role these CCPs play. The key role a CCP plays is the reduction of counterparty credit risk through a process called novation - the CCP steps between each trade acting as the buyer to every seller and the seller to every buyer. In this process, it concentrates the counterparty credit risk onto itself, and to protect against these default risks it collects collateral from investors and maintains a default fund. 

While risk in this new system is transformed, it does not disappear. The concentration of risk onto the CCP brings with it concerns of CCP failure - an event which could prove catastrophic. The possibility is not without precedent, with a large client default at Swedish Nasdaq Clearing AB in September 2018 leading to a near default of the CCP - out of a default fund of 166 million euros, 107 million euros were used. Clearing member defaults are dealt with by a "default waterfall". The first layer is formed by Initial Margin (IM) and Variation Margin (VM) - with the IM collected to account for potential future changes during a margin period of risk, and the VM to adjust for Mark-to-Market gains and losses on the position. The second layer, to be used if the IM proves insufficient, is the default fund contribution of the defaulted member. The third layer is a part of the CCPs own resources, referred to as the CCP's "Skin In The Game (SITG)". The fourth layer is the default fund contribution of non-defaulting members, and if this proves inadequate the CCP enters a state of recovery. 

The reduction of counterparty risk using collateral and margins, however, does bring with it increased liquidity risks. These risks can be pro-cyclical in nature -- increased margin requirements due to price changes or increases in volatility need to be met. Firms may need to acquire offsetting positions or conduct a fire sale of assets to fulfil these liquidity needs, leading to price impact and price changes which lead to further margin calls \citep{blanck_2025}. For reference, in the wake of the COVID-19 crisis, initial margins increased by about a third of pre-crisis levels at the four largest UK and EU CCPs due to recalculations based on increased market volatility \citep{esrb_2020}.

The shift towards central clearing has brought with it countless studies evaluating the benefits the CCPs bring, the liquidity risks that arise from them, the adequacy of margining models and event studies of stress episodes. Studies on the working of the client clearing market, however, remain scarce \citep{menkveld_vuillemey_2021}. Clearing membership at a CCP comes with countless requirements, and members at these CCPs are mostly larger banks and specialised Non-Bank Clearing Members. Other market participants, such as hedge funds, pension funds, small banks and proprietary trading shops become clients of these Clearing Members that offer client clearing services. The legal obligation of the client's position then falls upon the clearing member, which passes along margin requirements onto its client. 

This paper proposes an Agent-Based Model (ABM) to explore the role and effect of client clearing on CCP systemic risk. An ABM offers the ability to recreate the complex and reactive behaviours of market participants and central clearing, and can do so without the need for a proprietary clearing dataset. The ABM is built upon a financial market simulation calibrated to reproduce the stylised facts of returns we see in the market. The traders in this financial market simulation consist of the Bank Clearing Members and clients which are then connected to a tiered CCP environment, with a framework for margin calls from market participants and realistic CCP default management. \\

\noindent This work therefore has two central goals and contributions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The design of a calibrated and realistic Agent-Based Model for the tiered nature of central clearing.
  \item The use of this proposed model to explore the procyclical nature of margin calls in stressed market environments.\\
\end{itemize}

\noindent The rest of the paper is organised as follows. Section \ref{ch:related} reviews the related literature on agent-based market microstructure, central clearing and CCP systemic risk, and agent-based model calibration. Section \ref{ch:implications} discusses the implications of these findings together with the limitations of the approach. Finally, Section \ref{ch:conclusion} concludes and outlines directions for future work.
