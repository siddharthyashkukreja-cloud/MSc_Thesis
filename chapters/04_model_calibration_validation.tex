% Agent-based models do not admit a likelihood function: the mapping from
% parameters to prices runs through millions of discrete order-book events,
% so classical estimation is unavailable. The standard response, and the one
% adopted here, is \emph{simulated moment matching}: choose the behavioural
% parameters so that summary statistics of the simulated market are as close
% as possible to the same statistics measured in real data
% \citep{franke_westerhoff_2012,lamperti_2018,gao_2022_xgb}. This chapter
% describes the data, the division of parameters into measured, calibrated,
% and pinned sets, the construction of the loss function, the two independent
% optimisation methods, and the validation protocol that guards the result.
% The guiding principles are parsimony --- every parameter must earn its place
% --- and honesty about identification: where a parameter is weakly identified
% or sits at a bound, this is disclosed rather than smoothed over.

% %==============================================================================
% \section{Data and regimes}
% \label{sec:cal-data}

% The empirical basis is the E-mini S\&P~500 futures tape: one-minute bars of
% the front-month contract, rolled at volume crossover, restricted to regular
% trading hours, with the quote midpoint $(b_t + a_t)/2$ as the price series
% --- the same observable the simulator produces, so all comparisons are
% like-for-like. Two regimes are calibrated as fully independent problems. The
% \emph{calm} regime spans calendar year 2019 ($257$ sessions, roughly
% $10^5$ one-minute observations); the \emph{stressed} regime spans the
% COVID-19 crash window from late February to early April 2020 ($29$
% sessions). Regimes are calibrated separately because the two market states
% differ in volatility, book depth, and microstructure, and forcing shared
% parameters across them would trade fit for compromise; the regime-switch
% design follows the calm/stressed convention of the reference model
% \citep{simudyne_odd}. Returns are intraday log-returns of the mid;
% overnight (session-boundary) returns are excluded on both the empirical and
% simulated sides, with session boundaries read from the data --- real ES
% sessions vary in length, and a fixed-grid convention was found to leak
% overnight gaps into the intraday moments. A separate market-by-price
% (MBP-10) sample of order-book events supports the direct measurement of
% placement depth.

% \section{Measured, calibrated, and pinned parameters}
% \label{sec:cal-split}

% Parameters enter the model through three doors, and only one of them
% involves optimisation.

% \emph{Measured offline.} Everything that can be estimated directly from
% data, is: the fundamental-value process and its local volatility
% (Section~3.\ref{sec:model-fv}); the order-placement depth parameter
% $p_{\mathrm{zi}}$, fitted by maximum likelihood to the distance-from-mid
% distribution of new limit orders in the MBP-10 data (a one-parameter
% geometric law is compared against a two-parameter log-normal by likelihood
% and retained); and the loss-absorbing capital of members and clients, taken
% from public FCM financial data and CCP disclosures. None of these re-enters
% the calibration loop, with one disclosed exception: under stress the
% placement parameter is calibrated rather than fixed, because the ablation
% study showed a sparser book materially improves the stressed fit while the
% calm fit prefers the directly measured value.

% \emph{Pinned by design.} Structural choices follow their references rather
% than the optimiser: the momentum horizon $\lambda$ is fixed externally as in
% \citet{majewski_2018}, who pin theirs to the six-month horizon implied by
% CTA-index correlations; the zero-intelligence market-order rate stays at the
% reference model's baseline value \citep{simudyne_odd}, structured after
% \citet{cont_stoikov_talreja_2008} (the ablation found calibrating it changes
% the fit negligibly); fundamental and momentum traders act every
% step, per the reference step sequence \citep{simudyne_odd}; and all
% clearing-tier constants are regulatory pins
% (Chapter~3.\ref{sec:model-margin}).

% \emph{Calibrated.} What remains is a deliberately small behavioural set,
% regime-specific: in the calm regime the three-dimensional vector
% $\theta = (c_f,\ \alpha_{\mathrm{zi}},\ \delta_{\mathrm{zi}})$ --- the
% fundamental-trader belief-width scale and the zero-intelligence limit-order
% arrival and cancellation rates --- and under stress the same set plus
% $p_{\mathrm{zi}}$, four dimensions in all. Search bounds are
% literature-anchored: the ZI rates are confined to ranges of the same order
% as their \citet{cont_stoikov_talreja_2008} baselines (an early unconstrained
% run drove the market-order rate to twenty times its reference value,
% producing a book-sweeping artefact), and the belief-width box was tightened
% after diagnostic runs located the economically sensible basin.

% \subsection{The stylised-fact battery}

% The calibration targets are the stylised facts of high-frequency returns
% \citep{cont_2001}, the statistical fingerprints that any credible simulated
% market must reproduce. In plain terms: returns are \emph{fat-tailed} (large
% moves occur orders of magnitude more often than a Gaussian allows); returns
% are \emph{linearly unpredictable} (the autocorrelation of returns is
% near zero from the first lag --- the price is close to a martingale); and
% volatility \emph{clusters} (the autocorrelation of absolute returns is
% positive and decays slowly --- turbulent minutes follow turbulent minutes).
% These three facts are encoded in five moment groups, computed identically
% on simulated and empirical intraday mid returns:

% \begin{itemize}
%   \item \textbf{KS} --- the two-sample Kolmogorov--Smirnov statistic
%         $\sup_x |\widehat F_{\mathrm{sim}}(x) - \widehat F_{\mathrm{emp}}(x)|$
%         between the simulated and empirical return distributions, the
%         whole-distribution target of \citet{gao_2022_xgb}. Intuitively, it
%         is the largest vertical gap between the two cumulative distribution
%         curves: zero would mean the simulated return distribution is
%         indistinguishable from the real one.
%   \item \textbf{V} --- the return standard deviation, anchoring the overall
%         volatility scale \citep{gao_2022_hfabm}.
%   \item \textbf{ACF1} --- the return autocorrelation at lags
%         $\{1, 5, 10, 20\}$ minutes, each smoothed over three adjacent lags
%         as in \citet{gao_2022_xgb} (whose lag centres $\{1,10,20\}$ are
%         extended here with a five-minute centre); its target is the
%         empirical near-zero profile, so this group penalises any mechanical
%         predictability the agents might introduce (e.g.\ bid--ask bounce or
%         trend-chasing).
%   \item \textbf{ACF2} --- the \emph{absolute}-return autocorrelation at the
%         same lags: the volatility-clustering target. Absolute returns are
%         used instead of squared returns deliberately: $r^2$ is dominated by
%         a handful of extreme observations, which makes its autocorrelation
%         so noisy that a clustering miss would vanish under the
%         standardisation below; $|r|$ carries the same signal with far less
%         sampling noise \citep{cont_2001}.
%   \item \textbf{Hill} --- the \citet{hill_1975} tail index, estimated on
%         the upper tail of $|r|$ and averaged over a band of tail fractions
%         for stability. The index $\alpha$ summarises how fast the tail
%         decays ($\Pr(|r|>x)\sim x^{-\alpha}$): empirical equity-index
%         returns sit near $\alpha \approx 3$, the ``cubic law''; smaller
%         values mean fatter tails. Following \citet{gao_2022_hfabm}, Hill
%         rather than kurtosis is the tail target --- excess kurtosis is
%         tracked only as a diagnostic, because it is governed by the few
%         largest observations in the sample and matching it amounts to
%         chasing noise.
% \end{itemize}

% KS and Hill are paired deliberately. The ablation study
% (Section~\ref{sec:cal-validation}) shows each controls a failure mode the
% other cannot: with KS alone the optimiser can match the body of the
% distribution while the tail explodes; with Hill alone the tail is pinned
% but the body drifts. Together they constrain the distribution everywhere.

% \subsection{Aggregation: noise-standardised distances}

% The five groups mix units --- a standard deviation of order $10^{-4}$, a
% dimensionless KS statistic, autocorrelations, a tail index near $3$ --- so
% they cannot simply be added. Following the inverse-variability weighting of
% \citet{franke_westerhoff_2012}, each moment's distance is expressed in
% units of that moment's own \emph{sampling noise}:
% \begin{equation}
% \label{eq:loss}
% D(\theta) \;=\; \sum_{c \in \{\mathrm{KS, V, ACF1, ACF2, Hill}\}} \Delta_c(\theta),
% \qquad
% \Delta_c(\theta) \;=\; \frac{1}{|M_c|}\sum_{m \in M_c}
% \frac{\bigl|\,\widehat m_{\mathrm{sim}}(\theta) - \widehat m_{\mathrm{emp}}\,\bigr|}{s_m},
% \end{equation}
% where $s_m$ is the empirical sampling standard deviation of moment $m$,
% estimated once per regime by a moving-block bootstrap \citep{kunsch_1989}
% on the historical returns, with block length equal to one full session ---
% longer than the longest autocorrelation lag in the battery, so resampling
% preserves the dependence structure the ACF moments measure. The intuition
% is that $|\widehat m_{\mathrm{sim}} - \widehat m_{\mathrm{emp}}|/s_m$ is a
% $z$-statistic: a value of two means the simulated moment misses its target
% by about two standard errors of what historical sampling variation alone
% could produce, and $D$ counts such standardised misses across the battery.
% This is the diagonal, absolute-deviation member of the
% \citet{franke_westerhoff_2012} family: the full inverse-covariance
% weighting is ill-conditioned with this many moments and one historical
% sample, inverse-\emph{variance} weights are scale-pathological here (the
% tiny sampling variance of the return standard deviation would inflate its
% component by orders of magnitude), and raw equal weights would let the
% large-scale moments bury the small ones.

% One property of \eqref{eq:loss} is recorded for honest reporting: the KS
% statistic is a two-sample distance whose sampling scale shrinks with the
% \emph{simulated} sample size, so $s_{\mathrm{KS}}$ is computed at the
% simulation's sample size and $D$ values are comparable only between runs
% with the same number of simulated days and seeds. All headline comparisons
% in the results chapter therefore hold the seed count fixed.

% The loss \eqref{eq:loss} is minimised twice, by two methods from the same
% research line, and the result is accepted only if they agree. The logic of
% the pairing: the grid is transparent and exhaustive but expensive and
% coarse; the surrogate is efficient and fine-grained but introduces an
% approximation layer. Each covers the other's weakness.

% \subsection{Exhaustive grid search}

% The first method evaluates the true simulator on a regular grid over the
% parameter box and takes the minimiser --- precisely the calibration of
% \citet{gao_2023}, who fit their Chiarella--Heston model by grid search on
% the same kind of stylised-fact distance. Each node is simulated over
% multiple seeds and several trading weeks, and the loss is computed on the
% pooled returns. At three to four dimensions this is computationally
% feasible and has two virtues that matter for a thesis: it cannot be misled
% by an approximation (every evaluation is the real model), and it returns
% the \emph{entire loss surface}, so identification --- which parameters the
% data actually pins down, and which directions are flat --- can be read
% directly off the output rather than asserted.
% % [NUMBERS: grid resolution per regime (D65 re-lock: calm 5^4=625, stressed

% \subsection{Surrogate-assisted simulated moment matching}

% The second method is the machine-learning-surrogate approach of
% \citet{gao_2022_hfabm} and \citet{gao_2022_xgb}, with the same two-stage
% structure (and the broader logic of surrogate calibration for ABMs
% introduced by \citealp{lamperti_2018}). The motivating problem is cost: one
% evaluation of $D(\theta)$ requires simulating weeks of market activity over
% several seeds, so the budget of true evaluations is a few hundred, far too
% few to optimise directly. The surrogate procedure spends that budget
% strategically:

% \begin{enumerate}
%   \item \emph{Design.} Sample the parameter box with a low-discrepancy
%         (Sobol) design --- points spread to cover the box evenly, which
%         beats random or Latin-hypercube sampling at small budgets --- and
%         run the true simulator at each point.
%   \item \emph{Surrogate.} Train a gradient-boosted tree regressor
%         \citep{chen_guestrin_2016} to predict the scalar loss,
%         $\widehat D: \theta \mapsto D$. A single model for the aggregate
%         loss is used, rather than one per moment, because one smooth(er)
%         scalar surface is more learnable from few points than twenty noisy
%         per-moment surfaces; its held-out $R^2$ is the running check that
%         the surrogate can be trusted at all. Loss labels are clipped at a
%         high percentile so a few catastrophic parameter draws do not
%         dominate the fit \citep{gao_2022_xgb}.
%   \item \emph{Active learning.} For a small number of rounds, score a large
%         candidate pool with the surrogate and run the true simulator on a
%         roughly 2:1 mix of \emph{exploit} points (lowest predicted loss)
%         and \emph{explore} points (random), retraining after each round
%         \citep{gao_2022_xgb} --- concentrating expensive simulations where
%         the optimum is likely to be, while keeping enough exploration to
%         escape a misled surrogate.
%   \item \emph{Stage-1 optimum.} Minimise the surrogate by dense evaluation
%         over a Sobol pool and take the argmin. Gradient methods are
%         deliberately avoided: a tree ensemble is piecewise constant, so its
%         gradients are zero or undefined almost everywhere.
%   \item \emph{Stage-2 refinement.} Around the surrogate optimum, evaluate
%         the \emph{true} simulator on a tight local design and select the
%         best true-loss point \citep{gao_2022_hfabm} --- correcting for
%         surrogate approximation error in the final answer.
%   \item \emph{Out-of-sample validation.} Re-simulate the selected
%         $\theta^\ast$ on seeds never used in the search and report the loss
%         and the full moment comparison at those fresh seeds.
% \end{enumerate}

% In plain terms: the simulator is expensive, so a cheap statistical copy of
% the loss surface is learned, used to decide where to look, and then the
% \emph{real} simulator has the final word both in the local refinement and
% in validation.

% \section{Validation protocol}
% \label{sec:cal-validation}

% Four layers of validation guard the calibration, in increasing order of
% scope.

% \paragraph{Fresh-seed re-ranking (selection noise).}
% A grid minimiser is an argmin over hundreds of noisy few-seed loss
% estimates, so the winning node is partly the \emph{luckiest} node and its
% reported loss is biased low --- a winner's curse. After every grid run, the
% top nodes are re-evaluated on fresh, common random seeds and re-ranked; the
% calibration is accepted only if the optimum survives re-ranking within
% noise. The same concern motivated rejecting an apparent improvement during
% the robustness campaign (below): a configuration that looked markedly
% better at screening resolution failed to replicate at higher resolution,
% and the episode is reported as a methodological caution --- a high
% surrogate $R^2$ does not guarantee a reproducible optimum; only
% replication across seeds does.

% \paragraph{Moment-level validation.}
% At the accepted optimum, the full moment battery is compared to its targets
% at out-of-sample seeds --- including the diagnostic moments excluded from
% the loss (kurtosis) --- and long-horizon checks aggregate the simulated
% path to daily frequency to verify the fit does not hold at one minute by
% distorting coarser scales. One known limitation is stated up front: the
% absolute-return autocorrelation is matched at short horizons but decays too
% quickly beyond roughly half an hour, the structural signature of a
% single-timescale momentum sector; multi-scale volatility memory requires
% mechanisms outside this model class \citep{cont_2001}, and the attempt to
% revive it with a second momentum cohort failed (below).

% \paragraph{Ablation study (design validation).}
% Each load-bearing design choice is validated by switching it off (or
% pinning it) in an otherwise identical calibration and re-measuring the
% loss: twelve cells covering the fundamental process (data-derived versus
% synthetic), the presence of the market maker and volatility trader, the
% pinning of each calibrated parameter in turn, and the composition of the
% loss itself (KS-only and Hill-only variants). The ablation answers, choice
% by choice, the examiner's question ``would the model be worse without
% this?'' --- and its headline findings shaped the final design: the
% belief-width scale $c_f$ is the dominant tail lever and must be calibrated;
% the KS--Hill pairing is necessary in both directions; the market maker
% hurts both regimes; the data-derived fundamental beats the synthetic one
% once the tail is controlled.
% % [NUMBERS: ablation table C0–C11 from AGENT.md / output, with per-cell D]

% \paragraph{Robustness campaign (parsimony validation).}
% Finally, a campaign of richer model variants tested whether the small
% calibrated set leaves systematic fit on the table: activation and
% cancellation gates for the fundamental and momentum traders, a second
% momentum cohort and larger momentum populations, a calibrated momentum
% horizon, combinations thereof, and calibration with the clearing tier
% switched on. The verdict is a parsimony result, reported as a finding
% rather than a failure: the multi-parameter extensions do not earn their
% parameters --- their apparent gains either fail to replicate across seeds
% or add estimation variance without fit --- and the only consistent
% improvement (a calibrated momentum horizon under stress) is small enough
% that the externally pinned horizon is retained on parsimony grounds. One
% campaign result doubles as a scope condition for the clearing chapters:
% calibrating with the clearing tier \emph{active} leaves the calm fit
% unchanged but materially shifts the stressed dynamics, i.e.\ the
% market-layer calibration is not invariant to clearing feedbacks under
% stress. The baseline is therefore calibrated bare-market --- matching the
% reference models, and keeping the clearing tier's parameters out of the
% behavioural loop --- and this non-invariance is flagged wherever stressed
% clearing results are interpreted.
