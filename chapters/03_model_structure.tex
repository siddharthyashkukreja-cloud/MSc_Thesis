The agent-based model is built in two layers. The first layer models a financial market simulation -- heterogenous trader agents putting in orders onto an exchange, operating a Limit Order Book (LOB) at a one minute frequency. The proxy asset is the E-mini S\&P 500 (ES) index futures, the most liquid future, and data from this asset is used to calibrate the market simulation. The type of asset also affects the choices we make in the clearing layer, applying regulatory guidelines that apply to ES trading and clearing. The second layer is clearing, in which a CCP novates the trades that executed on the exchange -- collecting margin calls and managing defaults. The two tiers are connected: price changes and orders in the market layer drive margin calls, trading freezes due to capital ratio requirements, and forced liquidations that impact price and liquidity, creating a loop that spreads contagion. 

In the spirit of \cite{chiarella_iori_perello_2009} and \cite{majewski_2018}, the market simulation is populated by fundamental traders, momentum traders and noise traders and is calibrated to reproduce stylised facts of returns. While ES data is used here for calibration, the same approach can be applied to any dataset. The model includes 2 regimes - a calm regime and a stressed regime, with parameters calibrated separately for each. In this way, a stressful market environment is produced endogenously. 
Richer designs --- a split short/long-horizon momentum trader, uniform- and inventory-skewed market makers,
and a volatility-sensitive trader --- were trialled but added complexity without improving the fit,
and were dropped.  The traders are either Bank Clearing Members trading on their house account, or clients of other Bank and Non-Bank Clearing Members. The clearing layer takes inspiration from the CCP risk model of \cite{deloitte_ccp} and extends it with the client layer, with design and constants based on regulation and public CCP disclosures.

\section{Financial Market Simulation}
\label{sec:model-market}

This section presents the design of the financial market simulation:-

\subsection{Limit Order Book}

The market consists of a single asset discrete time Limit Order Book (LOB), with each step corresponding to one minute of trading. At every one minute step, agents submit a limit or market order, with limit orders resting on the book till they are filled, cancelled, or replaced. Orders are matched by price-time priority, and the minimum tick-size corresponds to that of the E-mini: 0.25 index points equating to \$ 12.50 per contract. The mid-price is the average of the best bid and best ask after each auction,
\begin{equation}
  P_t = \frac{b_t + a_t}{2},
\end{equation}
where $b_t$ and $a_t$ are the best bid and best ask at the end of step $t$. The bid-ask spread and liquidity are a product of agent order placement, market environment, and trader balance sheets. The one minute frequency added the right balance in granularity to allow for straightforward calibration and adequate book depths with a lower amount of traders, and allow for longer runs without it being computationally prohibitive.

At a session boundary/new day the book is not cleared but repriced. The book is scaled and reopens at the new gapped fundamental with its shape intact. Momentum trader memory is also reset so that the overnight return does not enter into the momentum signal. This is a stylised simplification, replicating the exchange opening auction would add complexity and rebuilding from an empty book at session/day open would be disorderly. 

%------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Trading Agents}
\label{sec:agents}

The book is populated by 100 trading agents of three types - 40 fundamental traders (FT), 20 momentum traders (MT), and 40 noise traders/zero-intelligence traders (ZI). The ZIs serve as a constant source of liquidity to the book, the FTs keep asset price anchored to an exogenous fundamental signal, and the MTs amplify price movements and trade on trend. Ten FTs also serve as BCMs, and the remaining 30 alongside ZI and MT traders serve as clients of other BCMs and NBCMs. The trading strategies of each trader and explained below.  Order sizes are drawn uniformly from $[1, 10]$ for each trader. Each model order unit represents a block of ES contracts — 18 in the calm regime, 32 in the stressed — chosen such that the simulated per-minute volume matches the empirical ES volume ($\sim2,000$ and $\sim3,500$ contracts per minute).

\subsubsection{Fundamental Trader}

Fundamental traders trade based on the fundamental value (FV) of the stock, with the FV $V_t$ fed in as an exogenous signal. To introduce heterogeneity, each FT carries a fixed belief offset $z_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ drawn at initialisation, such that each FT carries its own private valuation. At each step, fundamental traders place a limit order at a reservation $R^{i}_t$ from the fundamental value (FV) signal:- 

\begin{equation}
  R^{i}_t = V_t\,\big(1 + z_i\,c_f\,\sigma_t\big),
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_t$ is the local volatility of the fundamental (Section~\ref{sec:fundamental}) - an EWMA of its squared returns - and
$c_f$ is a calibrated scale that sets the width of the belief
distribution.
The trader buys when its reservation sits above the mid-price and sells when it sits below, placing a limit order at its unique $R^{i}_t$. The heterogeneous $z_i$ being fixed for a trader at onset leads to high $z_i$ traders holding a more optimistic view that causes their positions to accumulate and build, and vice-versa for the low $z_i$ traders. This leads to concentrated inventories, allowing for meaningful exposures to margin calls and market stress. The belief-width scale $c_f$, calibrated per regime, sets how far reservations travel from the
fundamental and is a key lever for reproducing the stylised facts across the two regimes.


\subsubsection{Momentum Trader}

Momentum traders (MT) serve as trend followers and track an exponentially weighted moving
average of mid-price log-returns,
\begin{equation}
  M_t = (1-\lambda)\,M_{t-1} + \lambda\,\big(\log P_{t-1} - \log P_{t-2}\big),
\end{equation}
buying when the momentum signal $M_t > 0$ and selling when $M_t < 0$ - placing a limit order on that side at a geometric depth $k \sim \mathrm{Geometric}(p_d)$ from the last mid, a parameter shared with the noise traders. $\lambda$ dictates the time horizon of trend, and is calibrated per regime rather than
fixed. An MT does not trade every step: at each step it activates with probability
$\tanh\!\big(|M_t| / (\gamma_M\,\sigma_v)\big)$, so the share of momentum traders
that act rises with the strength of the prevailing trend, and the activation scale
$\gamma_M$ is calibrated alongside $\lambda$. In simple terms, the stronger the recent move relative to normal one-minute volatility, the larger the fraction of momentum traders that put in an order. An MT limit order stays on the book until it is filled or a new order is placed. $\lambda$ and $\gamma_M$ are calibrated as part of the surrogate modelling loop.



\subsubsection{Noise Trader}

Noise/zero-intelligence (ZI) traders place orders at random and provide the book's
background liquidity \citep{farmer_2005}. Following \cite{cont_stoikov_talreja_2008}, ZI order flow is modelled as unique rates for limit order, market order and order cancellation placements, following a Poisson process, corresponding to a per-step probability. A ZI trader submits a limit order with probability $\alpha_{zi}$ on a random side at a geometric
depth $k \sim \mathrm{Geometric}(p_d)$, submits a market order with probability $\mu_{zi}$ on a
random side, and cancels each resting order with probability $\delta_{zi}$. ZI orders stay on the book until they are filled or cancelled. The three rates $\alpha_{zi}$,
$\mu_{zi}$, and $\delta_{zi}$ are calibrated as part of the surrogate modelling loop, alongside $p_d$ which is shared with the momentum trader. 

\subsection{The Fundamental Value Process}
\label{sec:fundamental}

The fundamental value $V_t$ (FV) is exogenously created and fed as a signal to fundamental traders (FTs). By default, the fundamental value is taken as the empirical mid price series for E-mini (ES) futures as a proxy. A synthetic GBM with a mean reverting volatility process is kept as an alternative, and both lead to a realistic reproduction of stylised facts. As the simulated mid tracks the FV closely, our model also recreates a path similar to a historical calm or stressed period, and the synthetic FV allows for testing across a range of stressed stochastic paths. 


\paragraph{Data-driven signal.}
By default $V_t$ is the observed one-minute ES mid, and overnight gaps are maintained as they drive a large portion of returns and contribute to stress.

\paragraph{Synthetic alternative.}

The alternative replaces $V_t$ with a synthetic series rather than the data. Prices follow a geometric
Brownian motion with stochastic mean reverting volatility. Following the construction of \citet{barndorff_shephard_2001}, the volatility is a level $\sigma_d$ scaled by a
fast and a slow mean-reverting factor:
\begin{equation}
  \log V_{t+1} - \log V_t = \mu + \sigma_t\,\varepsilon_t,
  \qquad
  \sigma_t = \sigma_d\,\exp\!\big(f_t + s_t\big) .
\end{equation}
Each of $f_t$ (fast) and $s_t$ (slow) is an Ornstein--Uhlenbeck process that drifts back toward zero
at its own speed --- $f_t$ over minutes, giving intraday volatility bursts, and $s_t$ over days,
carrying volatility that persists across sessions --- and $\sigma_d$ is the measured volatility level. Compared to a single factor, this does a better job at reproducing volatility clustering. Merton jumps were also trialled, but the multiplicative volatility of this model was sufficient to reproduce heavy tails. Synthetic FV parameters such as fast to slow ratio and volatility level $\sigma_d$ are jointly calibrated with agent parameters, which to our knowledge is a novel application of the surrogate modelling approach, explained in Section~\ref{sec:cal-method}. The one minute drift $\mu$ is set to a target stressed drawdown of 30\% over a two month (42 session) stressed window. 


\paragraph{Local volatility.}
Each fundamental is accompanied by a local volatility $\sigma_t$ that feeds into the FT reservation. For the synthetic path this is simply the mean-reverting volatility above. For
the data-driven path it is an exponentially weighted moving average of $V_t$'s squared one-minute
returns,
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_t^2 = (1-\phi)\,\sigma_{t-1}^2 + \phi\,\big(\log V_t - \log V_{t-1}\big)^2,
\end{equation}
with the weight $\phi$ averaging over a short intraday window (30 minute half life) and the average reset at each session open so the overnight gap does not enter it. 

\paragraph{Overnight gaps.}

Both fundamentals carry an overnight gap at the session boundaries. In ES data, these overnight returns contribute to $\sim 40\%$ of return variance with the largest moves occurring overnight. The data driven FV path carries these gaps in sequence, and the synthetic path randomly draws one of these gaps from the regime's overnight returns. These gaps are also excluded when parameters are calibrated.

\section{Central Clearing Layer}

The clearing layer offers a simplified but realistic representation of CCP practises, complete with a margin cycle and a default waterfall framework. It guaranties the trades executed in the exchange layer of Section~\ref{sec:model-market}. The network consists of the CCP as a central node, connected to a tier of Bank Clearing Members (BCMs) and Non-Bank Clearing Members (NBCMs), similar to the CCP risk model of \cite{simudyne_odd}, and extends it with a layer of clients that clear through the NBCMs and some of the BCMs. Constants used in the clearing layer, such as balance sheet sizes of members and default fund and margin sizing, are based on regulatory standards or CCP disclosures. The clearing layer and the market layer are connected through a feedback loop; prices formed in the market layer drive margin calls and concentrated portfolios, leading to trading freezes or liquidations when clients and CMs approach capital ratios, which lead to higher transactions costs and lower liquidity in the market layer, further impacting balance sheets.  

%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Clearing Members and Topology}
\label{sec:clearing-topology}

The CCP is connected to a layer of 10 BCMs - out of which 5 offer client clearing -, and 5 NBCMs. The NBCMs do not trade, and the BCMs are modelled as Fundamental Traders. There are 90 clients, each attached to a NBCM or a BCM, modelled as a fundamental trader, momentum trader or noise trader (Figure~\ref{fig:clearing_topology}). The CCP novates each trade, and the CMs guarantee client positions and post margins on their behalf, relaying the margin calls down to them.

\subsubsection{Central Counterparty (CCP)}

The CCP novates every cleared trade, maintains the member registry and
the per-member default-fund accounts, issues the margin calls, and manages defaults in accordance with a five-level default waterfall. The CCP has its own capital of \$1.5\,billion, mimicking the scale of a large clearing house. It uses this capital to contribute to the default fund, a CCP \emph{skin-in-the-game} (SITG), sized at $3\%$ of the overall pooled default fund, based on observed amounts \citep{fia_tracker}. When a Clearing Member defaults, the CCP takes on their residual book (client books are ported to other CMs) and transfers it to a surviving member with the largest offsetting position at a discount, a simplification of the closeout auctions CCPs perform in reality. Specifics on the default management framework are laid out in Section~\ref{sec:clearing-margin}.

\subsubsection{Banking Clearing Members (BCMs)}

There are 10 total BCMs, all 10 trading a house account under FT logic \ref{sec:agents}, and 5 among these offer client clearing services. All BCMs carry institutional scale initial capital, drawn from  $U[\$5,\$10]$\ billion, in line with observed scales \citep{cftc_fcm}. The Basel III Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR) is used as a constraint for these members \citep{haynes_mcphail_zhu_2019,acosta_smith_2018}. They trade under the constraint  $\kappa = \mathrm{cash}/\mathrm{exposure} \ge 8\%$, where cash is net of posted margin and exposure is the member's own and its client cleared notional. This mimics Basel III Pillar-1 Capital requirement of holding 8\% of Risk-Weighted Assets as capital. Besides accumulated positions, higher margins also lower cash and hence add to the transmission of stress by tightening the capital ratio. A flat cap is also placed on each BCM's book relative to its capital.  A client-clearing BCM is held to $|N^{\mathrm{own}}_\tau| \le 2\,C_\tau$. A house-only
BCM carries no client notional, so the same multiple would pin its ratio far above the $8\%$ floor; it is instead given the looser $|N^{\mathrm{own}}_\tau| \le 5\,C_\tau$, enough for its
book to approach the floor under stress as a client-clearing member's does, without permitting an
excessive build-up. 

On breaching the $8\%$ floor, a BCM freezes trading and client clearing, and deleverages its own book until it returns to a $10\%$ capital ratio. As this deleveraging takes place on exchange, the BCM tries to limit price impact through an Almgren-Chriss optimal execution schedule \citep{almgren_chriss_2000}. 

\subsubsection{Non-Banking Clearing Members (NBCMs)}

The five NBCMs have a purely clearing function, they hold no proprietary positions and do not put in orders. Their capital is significantly lower than the BCMs, and drawn from $U[\$0.5\,,\$3]$\ billion, similar to observed levels  \citep{cftc_fcm}. The NBCMs forward margin calls to their clients, but are responsible for client shortfalls on default and the default fund contributions. They have the same CAR as the BCMs, but instead of deleveraging they simply freeze clearing services until the capital ratio moves back above the threshold. \cite{deloitte_ccp} follow a similar design for deleveraging and freezing between BCMs and NBCMs. 

\subsubsection{Clients}

The client layer is an explicit extension on the work of \cite{deloitte_ccp}. Ninety clients clear through the NBCMs and the five BCMs that offer client clearing. Each client is connected to a single CM, a simplification in line with \cite{ofr_2026} who find that the modal client only has one clearing connection.

Clients are assigned to clearing members by a capacity-proportional (leverage-balanced) rule. Clients are looked at in order of largest size, and assigned to a member for whom the resulting client book to capital ratio would be lowest. Each client clearing member then ends up with a similar ratio for their cash balance and client cash balance. In this way, the largest clients clear through the largest CMs. The resulting number of clients varies from 3 for a small NBCM to 16 for a large BCM.  Client capital is drawn by type --- fundamental clients
$U[\$0.5,\$3]$\,billion, momentum clients $U[\$0.2,\$1]$\,billion, and noise (zero-intelligence)
accounts $U[\$200,\$500]$\,million. Due to this, more of the large fundamental books end up at the banks and the NBCMs carry more of the noise traders. 

A client may take on a position up to its CM's house margin limit: set at a fixed 15\% of notional. This is a simplifying assumption to restrict large positions whose margin cannot be financed. This level is higher than the CCP's IM that falls within  $4$--$11\%$, proxying the higher requirements CMs require from their clients. Each margin cycle, variation margin is covered through their cash and initial margin is placed to the CCP through the clearing member. Furthermore, it also freezes trading if its own CAR goes to 4\%. This allows the client to take on more risk than the CMs. If it cannot meet a margin call using its cash, a client has defaulted. On account of a client default, its clearing member assumes its position and liquidates it over an Almgren-Chriss Schedule \citep{almgren_chriss_2000}, covering any residual shortfall over the clients' posted margins.

\subsection{Margin and Default Framework}
\label{sec:clearing-margin}

This subsection covers how margins - Initial and Variation - and Default Fund contributions are designed. It also covers how the CCP handles defaults. 

\subsubsection{Margin Calls}

Initial Margin (IM) is calculated based on a Clearing Member's gross exposure -- its own book alongside the gross notional of its client book -- and hence client positions are not netted by default. IM is priced with a Value at Risk (VaR) methodology, at a 99\%ile move over one day, a standard Margin Period of Risk (MPOR) for such a contract:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:im}
f_\tau =  \max\!\bigl(4\%,\; \alpha_{99}\,\sigma^{\mathrm{daily}}_\tau\,\sqrt{\mathrm{MPOR}}\bigr)\
\qquad \alpha_{99} = 3.0,\quad \mathrm{MPOR} = 1\ \text{day},
\qquad \mathrm{IM}_\tau = f_\tau \times \text{exposure}.
\end{equation}

 $\sigma^{\mathrm{daily}}_\tau$ is the daily return volatility calculated as an
exponentially-weighted average of recent daily returns --- the RiskMetrics
$\lambda\approx0.94$ convention, using daily price data including a pre-window period so it is available on simulation day one. Close to close returns are used to so the overnight gaps are included in IM calculation. MPOR of 1 day aligns with the CME convention for ES Mini contracts; and $\alpha_{99}=3.0$ is the
$99\%$ quantile of E-mini S\&P~500 returns based on a Filtered Historical Simulation, as returns are not always normally distributed. The $4\%$ floor is an anti-procyclicality minimum (EMIR Art.~28): in quiet markets the
raw VaR sits below it, so margin holds at $4\%$; in stress the VaR term takes over and the rate climbs
with volatility (to $\sim$11\%).  A cleared client
posts its own initial margin: the CCP's call passes down through its clearing member, the client
funds it from its own cash, and the member transfers it to the CCP.

IM is recalculated every hour, and is called in alongside the Variation Margin (VM) on a fixed hourly cycle. Mark-to-Market changes are called in as VM and settled, leading to cash outflows or inflows based on the current mid price.

\subsubsection{Liquidation and Porting}

A default is resolved differently by both layers. For a client default, its CM assumes its position and liquidates it on the open market, following an Almgren-Chriss schedule to limit price impact. The default of a Clearing Member is resolved by the CCP, it covers losses first through the defaulter's Initial Margin and transfers its position to the member with the largest opposing position at a recovery rate of 0.80. This is a simplified representation of the auction a CCP does with remaining members, and is meant to limit losses through the fire sales of large positions.

CM assumed positions are liquidated over a 30 minute horizon, sliced into Almgren-Chriss (AC) sized market orders to limit transitory price impact. The AC schedule front loads more aggressively in stressed periods due to higher volatility, and approaches near uniform (TWAP) liquidation in calm periods -- although here it rarely gets exercised. The impact coefficients are calibrated per regime from 1-min price and volume data, and the optimum liquidation urgency is derived using these parameters and a risk aversion parameter shared across regimes \citep{almgren_chriss_2000}. Permanent impact is empirically negative at this horizon, so the permanent impact is floored at zero and the market is treated as transient-impact
dominated at the one-minute horizon \citep{bouchaud_gefen_potters_wyart_2004}.

On a member default the CCP first tries to port the defaulter's surviving clients to other
client-clearing members that retain capital-ratio headroom after taking them on.
Porting is capacity-constrained: clients that cannot be placed are closed out and frozen.

\subsubsection{Default Fund}

For losses that exceed the IM, CCPs keep a Default Fund (DF) for which they collect contributions from their members. The DF in the model is based on a Cover-2 principle which is the new regulatory standard, the DF size should be large enough to cover the conjoint default of its two largest members by exposure. This is modelled as losses (L) beyond their own margin in an extreme two day move, at a higher confidence level than IM at $\sim$99.9\% of the same FHS volatility, a principle known as Stressed Loss over Initial Margin (SLOIM). The frequency of collections for default funds vary per CCP, from weekly to monthly to quarterly. The model being a short simplified simulation collects it daily.

The fund covers the two largest
shortfalls plus a buffer, and the exchange pre-funds a portion as
SITG (consumed ahead of the mutualised member pool, but after the defaulter's own
resources):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:df}
\mathrm{DF} = (1+b)\,\bigl(L_{(1)} + L_{(2)}\bigr), \qquad b = 10\%, \qquad
\mathrm{SITG} = 3\%\times\mathrm{DF},
\end{equation}
where $L_{(1)} \ge L_{(2)}$ are the two largest member shortfalls.  Each member pre-funds its share in
cash in proportion to its margin; a non-bank member's share rests entirely on its gross client
book. The SITG level is based on the amounts seen at major CCPs that sit around 1 to 5 \% \citep{cftc_fcm,fia_tracker}.


\subsubsection{Default Waterfall}

The resources a CCP uses to absorb a default form its default waterfall, modelled as in
Figure~\ref{fig:waterfall}. The defaulter's Initial Margin is seized first, then its own default-fund
contribution. Next comes the CCP's skin-in-the-game (SITG), and following that the mutualised pooled
default fund. Beyond the pooled fund the model draws on the surviving members' own cash, pro-rata and finally the CCP's remaining capital; only beyond these would the
CCP enter recovery (e.g.\ variation-margin gains haircutting), which is outside the model's scope. In
the stressed experiments, defaults reach the pooled default fund (L3) at most.
