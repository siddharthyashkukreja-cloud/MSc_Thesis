This section describes the Client Clearing Agent-Based model and its architecture. The model is built in two tiers - the first is a market simulation where heterogeneous agents trade an asset on a discrete Limit Order Book operating at a one minute frequency, and the second is a clearing layer in which a CCP novates these trades, collects margin calls and handles defaults. The two tiers are connected: price changes and orders in the market layer drive margin calls, trading freezes due to capital ratio requirements, and forced liquidations that impact price and liquidity, creating a loop that spreads contagion. In the spirit of \cite{chiarella_iori_perello_2009} and \cite{majewski_2018}, the market simulation is populated by fundamental traders, momentum traders and noise traders and is calibrated to reproduce stylised facts of returns. Other trader types were trialled to try and make the simulation more realistic. This included splitting the momentum trader into a short-term and long-term momentum trader, adding a Market Maker - both uniform quoting and inventory skewed quoting - and a trader that trades more actively in volatile market situations. These traders, however, did not lead to an improvement in the model and hence added unjustified complexity. 

The data used for calibration is E-mini S\&P 500 (ES) data, but the model can be calibrated to any other asset. The model includes 2 regimes - a calm regime and a stressed regime, with parameters calibrated separately for each. Parameter values and descriptions can be found in Appendix~\ref{ch:appendix}. In this way, a stressful market environment is produced endogenously. The traders are either Bank Clearing Members trading on their house account, or clients of other Bank and Non-Bank Clearing Members. The clearing layer takes inspiration from the CCP risk model of \cite{deloitte_ccp} and extends it with the client layer, with clearing layer parameters based on regulation and public CCP disclosures. The model is then used to study the effects of client clearing on systemic risk and the procyclicality of margin calls.

\section{Financial Market Simulation}
\label{sec:model-market}

This section presents the design of the financial market simulation:-

\subsection{Limit Order Book}

The market consists of a single asset discrete time Limit Order Book (LOB), with each step corresponding to one minute of trading. At every one minute step, agents submit a limit or market order, with limit orders resting on the book till they are filled, cancelled, or replaced. Orders are matched by price-time priority, and the minimum tick-size corresponds to that of the E-mini: 0.25 index points equating to \$ 12.50 per contract. The mid-price is the average of the best bid and best ask after each auction,
\begin{equation}
  P_t = \frac{b_t + a_t}{2},
\end{equation}
where $b_t$ and $a_t$ are the best bid and best ask at the end of step $t$. The bid-ask spread and liquidity are a product of agent order placement, market environment, and trader balance sheets. 

%------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Trading Agents}
\label{sec:agents}

The book is populated by 100 trading agents of three types - 40 fundamental traders (FT), 20 momentum traders (MT), and 40 noise traders/zero-intelligence traders (ZI). The ZIs serve as a constant source of liquidity to the book, the FTs keep asset price anchored to an exogenous fundamental signal, and the MTs amplify price movements and trade on trend. Ten of the FTs also serve as Bank Clearing Members (BCMs), and the remaining 30 and the ZI and MT traders serve as clients of other BCMs and Non-Bank Clearing Members (NBCMs). FTs and MTs place limit orders at each step, with new orders replacing old orders. ZIs place both limit and market orders, and order cancellations, leading to the presence of resting orders on the book. FTs place limit orders at a heterogeneous fundamental signal they observe, and MTs and ZIs place limit orders at a depth of $k$ steps from the previous mid, drawn from a geometric distribution $k \sim \mathrm{Geometric}(p_d)$, where $p_d$ can be fit to L2 order book or calibrated alongside other parameters, with the latter being employed for this model. A log-normal depth was trialled, but stylised facts were more easily reproduced with a geometric depth of limit order placement.  Order sizes are drawn uniformly from $[1, 10]$ for each trader. Each model order unit represents a block of ES contracts — 18 in the calm regime, 32 in the stressed — chosen so that the simulated per-minute volume matches the empirical ES volume (≈2,000 and ≈3,500 contracts per minute).

\subsubsection{Fundamental Trader}

Fundamental traders trade based on the fundamental value of the stock. The fundamental value $V_t$ is fed as an exogenous signal. To introduce heterogeneity, each FT carries a fixed belief offset $z_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ drawn at initialisation, so each FT carries its own private valuation. At each step, fundamental traders place a limit order at a reservation $R^{i}_t$ from the fundamental value (FV) signal:- 

\begin{equation}
  R^{i}_t = V_t\,\big(1 + z_i\,c_f\,\sigma_t\big),
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_t$ is the local volatility of the fundamental (Section~\ref{sec:fundamental}) - an EWMA of its squared returns - and
$c_f$ is a calibrated scale that sets the width of the belief
distribution.
The trader buys when its reservation sits above the mid-price and sells when it sits below, placing a limit order at its unique $R^{i}_t$. The heterogeneous $z_i$ being fixed for a trader at onset leads to high $z_i$ traders holding a more optimistic view that causes their positions to accumulate and build, and vice-versa for the low $z_i$ traders. This leads to concentrated inventories, allowing for meaningful exposures to margin calls and market stress. The belief width scale $c_f$ is calibrated as a parameter and controls how far reservations travel from FV and acts as a meaningful lever in reproducing stylised facts, and allows for a more accurate model for the calm and stressed period split. 


\subsubsection{Momentum Trader}

Momentum traders serve as trend followers and track an exponentially weighted moving
average of mid-price log-returns,
\begin{equation}
  M_t = (1-\lambda)\,M_{t-1} + \lambda\,\big(\log P_{t-1} - \log P_{t-2}\big),
\end{equation}
buying when the momentum signal $M_t > 0$ and selling when $M_t < 0$ - placing a limit order on that side at a geometric depth $k \sim \mathrm{Geometric}(p_d)$ from the last mid. $\lambda$ dictates the time horizon of trend, and is calibrated per regime rather than
fixed. An MT does not trade every step: at each step it activates with probability
$\tanh\!\big(|M_t| / (\gamma_M\,\sigma_v)\big)$, so the share of momentum traders
that act rises with the strength of the prevailing trend, and the activation scale
$\gamma_M$ is calibrated alongside $\lambda$. In simple terms, the stronger the recent move relative to normal one-minute volatility, the larger the fraction of momentum traders that put in an order.



\subsubsection{Noise Trader}

Noise, or zero-intelligence (ZI), traders place orders at random and provide the book's
background liquidity \citep{farmer_2005}. Following \cite{cont_stoikov_talreja_2008}, ZI order flow is modelled as unique rates for limit order, market order and order cancellation placements, following a Poisson process, corresponding to a per-step probability. 
 A ZI trader therefore submits a limit order with probability $\alpha_{zi}$ on a random side at a geometric
depth $k \sim \mathrm{Geometric}(p_d)$, submits a market order with probability $\mu_{zi}$ on a
random side, and cancels each resting order with probability $\delta_{zi}$. Unlike FT and MT, ZI orders stay on the book until they are filled or cancelled. The three rates $\alpha_{zi}$,
$\mu_{zi}$, and $\delta_{zi}$ are calibrated as part of the surrogate modelling loop.

\subsection{The Fundamental Value Process}
\label{sec:fundamental}

The fundamental value $V_t$ is exogenously created and fed as a signal to fundamental traders (FTs). By default, the fundamental value is taken as the empirical mid price series for E-mini (ES) futures. A synthetic GBM with jumps and a mean reverting volatility process is kept as an alternative, and both lead to a realistic reproduction of stylised facts.


\paragraph{Data-driven signal.}
By default $V_t$ is the observed one-minute ES mid, and overnight gaps are maintained as they drive a large portion of returns and contribute to stress. This also allows for the FV signal to directly replicate the properties of the calm and stressed period.

\paragraph{Synthetic alternative.}
The alternative approach replaces the real series with a synthetic series, a Geometric Brownian
Motion whose instantaneous volatility $\sigma_t$ follows a mean-reverting Ornstein--Uhlenbeck
process \citep{stein_stein_1991,heston_1993} overlaid with Merton jumps \citep{merton_1976}:
\begin{align}
  \sigma_{t+1}            &= \theta + e^{-\kappa}\,(\sigma_t - \theta) + \xi\,\varepsilon^{\sigma}_t, \\
  \log V_{t+1} - \log V_t &= \mu + \sigma_t\,\varepsilon_t + J_t,
      \qquad J_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_J^2).
\end{align}
The jumps $J_t$ arrive as a Poisson process---roughly two per session in the calm regime and one
in the stressed regime---with sizes drawn from a normal distribution whose standard deviation
$\sigma_J$ is small, about $0.1\%$ per jump in calm markets and $0.7\%$ under stress. The
mean-reverting volatility is the feature that generates realistic long-horizon volatility
clustering in the model.
\paragraph{Overnight gaps.}
Both fundamentals retain overnight gaps, which holds relevance as the largest moves in the stressed sample occurred overnight. The data-driven path carries the realised gaps
in sequence, while the synthetic path draws each gap from the regime's observed overnight returns.

\paragraph{Local volatility.}
Each fundamental is accompanied by a local volatility $\sigma_t$ that feeds into the FT reservation. For the synthetic path this is simply the mean-reverting volatility above. For
the data-driven path it is an exponentially weighted moving average of $V_t$'s squared one-minute
returns,
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_t^2 = (1-\phi)\,\sigma_{t-1}^2 + \phi\,\big(\log V_t - \log V_{t-1}\big)^2,
\end{equation}
with the weight $\phi$ averaging over a short intraday window and the average reset at each session open so the overnight gap does not enter it.

\section{Central Clearing Layer}

The clearing layer offers a simplified but realistic representation of CCP practises, complete with a margin cycle and a default waterfall framework. It guarantees the trades formed in the market of Section~\ref{sec:model-market}. The network is formed by the CCP as a central node, connected to a tier of Bank Clearing Members (BCMs) and Non-Bank Clearing Members (NBCMs), similar to the CCP risk model of \cite{simudyne_odd}, and extends it with a layer of clients that clear through the NBCMs and some of the BCMs. Constants used in the clearing layer, such as the balance sheet sizes of members and default fund and margin sizing are based on regulatory standards or CCP disclosures. The clearing layer and the market layer are connected through a feedback loop; prices formed in the market layer drive margin calls and concentrated portfolios, leading to trading freezes or liquidations when clients and CMs approach capital ratios, which lead to price impact and lower liquidity in the market layer, driving further margin calls. 

%------------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Clearing Members and Topology}
\label{sec:clearing-topology}

The CCP is connected to a layer of 10 BCMs - out of which 5 offer client clearing -, and 5 NBCMs. The NBCMs do not trade, and the BCMs are modelled as Fundamental Traders. There are 90 clients, each attached to a NBCM or a BCM, modelled as a fundamental trader, momentum trader or noise trader (Figure~\ref{fig:clearing_topology}). The CCP novates each trade, and the CMs guarantee client positions and post margins on their behalf, relaying the margin calls down to them.

\subsubsection{Central Counterparty (CCP)}

The CCP novates every cleared trade, maintains the member registry and
the per-member default-fund accounts, issues the margin calls, and manages defaults in accordance with a five-level default waterfall. The CCP has its own capital of \$1.5\,billion, mimicking the scale of a large clearing house. It uses this capital to contribute to the default fund, a CCP \emph{skin-in-the-game} (SITG), sized at $3\%$ of the overall pooled default fund \citep{fia_tracker}. When a Clearing Member defaults, the CCP takes on their residual book (client books are ported to other CMs) and transfers it to a surviving member with the largest offsetting position at a discount, a simplification of the closeout auctions CCPs perform in reality. Specifics on the default management framework are laid out in (Section~\ref{sec:model-default}).

\subsubsection{Banking Clearing Members (BCMs)}

There are 10 total BCMs, all 10 trading a house account under the fundamental trader logic \ref{sec:agents}, and 5 among these offer client clearing services. All BCMs carry institutional scale initial capital, drawn from  $U[\$5,\$10]$\ billion, in line with observed scales \citep{cftc_fcm}. The Basel III Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR) is used as a constraint for these members \citep{haynes_mcphail_zhu_2019,acosta_smith_2018}. They trade under the constraint  $\kappa = \mathrm{cash}/\mathrm{exposure} \ge 8\%$, where cash is net of posted margin and exposure is the member's own and its client cleared notional. This mimics Basel III Pillar-1 Capital requirement of holding 8\% of Risk-Weighted Assets as Tier 1 capital. Besides accumulated positions, higher margins also lower cash and hence add to the transmission of stress by tightening the capital ratio. To restrict unbounded positions in one direction by these BCMs, an additional constraint is established: $|N^{\mathrm{own}}_\tau| \le 2\,C_\tau$ --- a flat cap  in the
spirit of a trading-book risk limit \citep{basel_frtb}. The limit is set such that the CAR stays comfortably above the constraint in calm periods and only approaches it during stressed periods.

On breaching the $8\%$ floor, a BCM freezes  trading and client clearing, and deleverages its own book until it returns to a $10\%$ capital ratio. As this deleveraging takes place on exchange, the BCM tries to limit price impact through an Almgren-Chriss optimal execution schedule \citep{almgren_chriss_2000}. 

\subsubsection{Non-Banking Clearing Members (NBCMs)}

The five NBCMs have a purely clearing function, they hold no proprietary positions and do not put in orders. Their capital is significantly lower than the BCMs, and drawn from $U[\$0.5\,,\$3]$\ billion, similar to observed levels  \citep{cftc_fcm}. The NBCMs forward margin calls to their clients, but are responsible for client shortfalls on default and the default fund contributions. They have the same CAR as the BCMs, but instead of deleveraging they simply freeze clearing services until the capital ratio moves back above the threshold. \cite{simudyne_odd} follow a similar design for deleveraging and freezing between BCMs and NBCMs. 

\subsubsection{Clients}

The client layer is an explicit extension on the work of \cite{deloitte_ccp}. Ninety clients - thirty fundamental traders, twenty momentum traders, and forty noise traders - clear through the NBCMs and the five BCMs that offer client clearing. Each client is connected to a single CM, a simplification in line with \cite{ofr_2026} who find that the modal client only has one clearing connection.

Clients are assigned to clearing members by a capacity-proportional (leverage-balanced) rule. Clients are looked at in order of largest size, and assigned to a member for whom the resulting client book to capital ratio would be lowest. Each client clearing member then ends up with a similar ratio for their cash balance and client cash balance. In this way, the largest clients clear through the largest CMs. The resulting number of clients varies from 3 for a small NBCM to 16 for a large BCM.  Client capital is drawn by type --- fundamental clients
$U[\$0.5,\$3]$\,billion, momentum clients $U[\$0.2,\$1]$\,billion, and noise (zero-intelligence)
accounts $U[\$200,\$500]$\,million. Due to this, more of the large fundamental books end up at the banks and the NBCMs carry more of the noise traders. 

A client may take on a position up to its CM's house margin limit: set at a fixed 15\% of notional. This is a simplifying assumption to restrict large positions whose margin cannot be financed, based on realistic margin levels for client accounts. Each margin cycle, variation margin is covered through their cash and initial margin is placed to the CCP through the clearing member. Furthermore, it also freezes trading if its own CAR goes to 4\%. This allows the client to take on more risk than the CMs. If it cannot meet a margin call using its cash, a client has defaulted. On account of a client default, its clearing member assumes its position and liquidates it over an Almgren-Chriss Schedule \citep{almgren_chriss_2000}, covering any residual shortfall over the clients' posted margins.

\subsection{Margin and Default Framework}
\label{sec:clearing-margin}

This subsection covers how margins - Initial and Variation - and Default Fund contributions are designed. It also covers how the CCP handles defaults. 

\subsubsection{Margin Calls}

Initial Margin (IM) is calculated based on a Clearing Member's gross exposure -- its own book alongside the gross notional of its client book -- and hence client positions are not netted by default. IM is priced with a Value at Risk (VaR) methodology, at a 99\%ile move over one day, a standard Margin Period of Risk (MPOR) for such a contract:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:im}
f_\tau =  \max\!\bigl(4\%,\; \alpha_{99}\,\sigma^{\mathrm{daily}}_\tau\,\sqrt{\mathrm{MPOR}}\bigr)\
\qquad \alpha_{99} = 3.0,\quad \mathrm{MPOR} = 1\ \text{day},
\qquad \mathrm{IM}_\tau = f_\tau \times \text{exposure}.
\end{equation}

 $\sigma^{\mathrm{daily}}_\tau$ is the daily return volatility calculated as an
exponentially-weighted average of recent daily returns --- the RiskMetrics
$\lambda\approx0.94$ convention, using daily price data including a pre-window period so it is available on simulation day one. MPOR of 1 day aligns with the CME convention for ES Mini contracts; and $\alpha_{99}=3.0$ is the
$99\%$ quantile of E-mini S\&P~500 returns based on a Filtered Historical Simulation, as returns are not always normally distributed. The $4\%$ floor is an anti-procyclicality minimum (EMIR Art.~28): in quiet markets the
raw VaR sits below it, so margin holds at $4\%$; in stress the VaR term takes over and the rate climbs
with volatility (to $\sim$11\%).  A cleared client
posts its own initial margin: the CCP's call passes down through its clearing member, the client
funds it from its own cash, and the member transfers it to the CCP.

IM is recalculated every hour, and is called in alongside the Variation Margin (VM) on a fixed hourly cycle. Mark-to-Market changes are called in as VM and settled, leading to cash outflows or inflows based on the current mid price. 
% ==========================================================================

\subsubsection{Liquidation and Porting}

A default is resolved differently by both layers. For a client default, its CM assumes its position and liquidates it on the open market, following an Almgren-Chriss schedule to limit price impact. The default of a Clearing Member is resolved by the CCP, it covers losses first through the defaulter's Initial Margin and transfers its position to the member with the largest opposing position at a recovery rate of 0.80. This is a simplified representation of the auction a CCP does with remaining members, and is meant to limit losses through the fire sales of large positions. The Almgren-Chriss schedule also differs in the stressed and calm period, front loading more aggressively in stressed due to higher volatility, and with near uniform (TWAP) liquidation in calm periods -- although here it rarely gets exercised. The impact coefficients are calibrated per regime from 1-min price and volume data, the temporary impact coefficient from a regression of $|r|$ on traded volume and the permanent coefficient from a regression of forward cumulative returns on signed volume, and the optimum liquidation urgency is derived using these parameters and a risk aversion parameter shared across regimes \citep{almgren_chriss_2000}. Permanent impact is empirically negative (prices revert beyond the trade), so the permanent impact is floored at zero and the market is treated as transient-impact
dominated at the one-minute horizon \citep{bouchaud_gefen_potters_wyart_2004}.

On a member default the CCP first tries to port the defaulter's surviving clients to other
client-clearing members that retain capital-ratio headroom after taking them on.
Porting is capacity-constrained: clients that cannot be placed are closed out and frozen.

\subsubsection{Default Fund}

For losses that exceed the IM, CCPs keep a Default Fund (DF) for which they collect contributions from their members. The DF in the model is based on a Cover-2 principle which is the new regulatory standard, the DF size should be large enough to cover the conjoint default of its two largest members by exposure. This is modelled as losses (L) beyond their own margin in an extreme two day move, at a higher confidence level than IM at $\sim$99.9\% of the same FHS volatility, a principle known as Stressed Loss over Initial Margin (SLOIM). The frequency of collections for default funds vary per CCP, from weekly to monthly to quarterly. The model being a short simplified simulation collects it daily.

The fund covers the two largest
shortfalls plus a buffer, and the exchange pre-funds a portion as
SITG (consumed ahead of the mutualised member pool, but after the defaulter's own
resources):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:df}
\mathrm{DF} = (1+b)\,\bigl(L_{(1)} + L_{(2)}\bigr), \qquad b = 10\%, \qquad
\mathrm{SITG} = 3\%\times\mathrm{DF},
\end{equation}
where $L_{(1)} \ge L_{(2)}$ are the two largest member shortfalls.  Each member pre-funds its share in
cash in proportion to its margin; a non-bank member's share rests entirely on its gross client
book. The SITG level is based on the amounts seen at major CCPs that sit around 1 to 5 \% \citep{cftc_fcm,fia_tracker}.


\subsubsection{Default Waterfall}

The resources CCPs use to protect against default, such as IM and Default Funds, are known as a CCPs default waterfall. The default waterfall is modelled as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:waterfall}. The first stage is the defaulter's Initial Margin, then their own default fund contribution. Next, a layer of CCP equity known as Skin-in-the-game (SITG) is used, and following that the mutualised pooled default fund. After that, the CCP enters a state of recovery, using more of its equity and practises such as VM gains haircutting, but these recovery steps are not part of the model. In extreme scenarios, the model reaches the pooled default fund.
