In this chapter, the Agent-Based Model for Client Clearing is presented across calm and stressed regimes. The standard model used the historical mid price series as a proxy for the Fundamental Value. The model is then demonstrated through a study of the procyclical nature of margin calls in stressed environments, comparing stress reactive margining to flat and constant margining. Finally, a version of the model is presented that replaces the empirical mid price fundamental value with a stochastic fundamental value, further expanding the usefulness of the model through simulations across various diverse paths.

This section simply presents the standard ABM as it is, across both regimes. The visualised mechanics of the model aid in a deeper understanding and a validation of the model's functions. 







This section presents the two-tier ABM for client clearing. It combines a calibrated LOB with realistic clearing practices and a CCP default waterfall. The descriptive figures illustrate the price generation, order flow, margin cycles and capital adequacies across the model.

\subsection{Capital Adequacy Ratios and Breaches}

The clearing members operate with CAR constraint of a minimum of 8\%. BCMs that carry clients operate close to this floor. In the stressed period, they also breach the CAR ratio and must subsequently deleverage, with about 3.1 of the 5 breaching in a typical stressed seed. The five proprietary BCMs stay well above the floor. The NBCMs display heterogeneous behaviour, 2 of the five generally breach in a stressed seed but with no way to deleverage, roughly 0.15 of the 5 default per seed. 

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering

  \caption{\textbf{Clearing-member capital ratio $\kappa$ --- individual members (representative seed)}}
  \label{fig:res-kappa}

Each stressed seed produces ≈8.3 client defaults (range 2–18; none in calm); on default the client's posted initial margin is seized first and the carrying member absorbs the residual variation-margin shortfall and close-out loss against its own capital, closing out the position — so with no member failure the loss is contained at the member and never reaches the mutualised default fund.

\subsection{Margin Cycle}

In calm periods, the IM stays at its anti-procyclicality floor of 4\%. Due to the low volatility and stable client trading, posted IM sees little variation. In stressed conditions, it starts at the 4\% floor and reaches a peak of 11.4\%, averaging 7.7\% across the window.

\section{Margin Procyclicality}

The model is applied to answer the single hypothesis of this thesis -- the procyclicality of margin calls. Risk and volatility sensitive margining practices are procyclical in nature: margins are low in calm markets and rise in stressed markets, demanding more collateral in the exact situation when it is hard to raise. This experiment switches the reactive volatility based IM for a flat IM across all regimes. This flat IM keeps it constant, and may include asking for an unnecessarily high buffer during calm times, and in return diminishing the need for procyclical margin calls. Three levels of margin are tested - a low level at 4\%, a level resembling the average stressed IM at 8\%, and a level covering the peak stressed IM at 12\%. There are many assets that already have IM requirements higher than 10\%, while ES futures tend to have low IM requirements due to their high liquidity. 

To test this hypothesis, the default fund (DF) had to be substituted. As the SLOIM default fund adjusts for IM, it shrank to zero for the high flat IMs that exceeded the extreme stress moves. A simple DF is used across both flat and reactive IMs, sized at a 10\% loss of the 2 largest members' positions. This leads to a DF size of $\approx\$12$--$14$B, similar to CME Clear levels. As previously mentioned, for reactive IM, the charged fraction
ramps $4\%\!\to\!11.4\%$ ($2.84\times$) into the crash (Figure~\ref{fig:res-im}).

The central finding is that flat margin requirements do not remove default risk, but instead relocate it. As flat IM increases, client defaults fall as with more margin, less of client cash is exhausted. At the same time, member defaults rise to an average of 0.75 per seed. The mutualised default fund is subsequently reached in a high proportion of seeds. The high margins drain the cash balances needed by the Clearing Members to absorb client defaults. Higher margins therefore lower client defaults, but at same time increase the likelihood of systemic and CM failure. The reactive IM, conversely, offers lower client and member defaults than the flat 8\% IM.
